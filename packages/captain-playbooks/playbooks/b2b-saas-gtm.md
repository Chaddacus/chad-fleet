# B2B SaaS Go-to-Market for Indie Builders

## Summary

Covers the GTM fundamentals for a single-founder or small-team B2B SaaS targeting $50K-$200K
ARR: ICP definition, pricing tier design, onboarding-to-conversion mechanics, channel selection,
and churn prevention. Fires when a new product is entering the market or an existing product
has stalled growth.

## When to consult

- A new B2B SaaS product is being prepared for its first 10 paying customers.
- Monthly growth has been flat for 2+ months without a root cause identified.
- Pricing is being set for the first time or redesigned after initial market feedback.
- Freemium tier is generating signups but no paid conversions.
- Monthly churn is above 3% MRR without a root cause identified.
- A new GTM channel (cold outbound, SEO, partnerships) is being evaluated.
- MRR has stalled below $10K for more than 60 days.

## Recommendations

### ICP definition

1. **Define your ICP around job-to-be-done, not firmographic demographics.**

   A job title and company size is a weak ICP. The right ICP answers three questions:
   (a) What specific outcome does the buyer need that they cannot easily get without your tool?
   (b) What does "good enough" look like to them, in their language?
   (c) Where do concentrations of these people show up at scale?

   "VP of Engineering at a 50-200 person SaaS company" is too broad. "VP of Engineering at a
   Series A SaaS company who manages CI/CD manually and has just had a production incident
   from a botched deploy" is actionable.

   Measurable outcome: the ICP definition in 3 sentences cannot describe a competitor's
   customer equally well.

2. **Validate willingness to pay before building the paywall.**

   Before launching a paid tier, talk to 5 potential customers. Ask: "If this saved you X hours
   per week, what would that be worth to your team per month?" Listen for the number they
   volunteer — it is almost always higher than what you planned to charge.

   If five customers volunteer $50/month and you planned $200/month, you have a positioning
   problem. If they say $400/month without prompting, you are underpricing.

   Measurable outcome: 5 discovery conversations logged with explicit willingness-to-pay
   signals before the first pricing page is published.

3. **Identify where your ICP concentrates and goes to learn.**

   Where do they ask for tool recommendations? (Slack communities, subreddits, LinkedIn groups,
   industry newsletters.) Where do they go when a specific problem fires? (Google search terms,
   YouTube, documentation sites.)

   Rank each concentration by: (a) cost to reach, (b) intent level — actively searching vs.
   passively scrolling.

   Measurable outcome: a list of 3-5 specific channels where your ICP asks questions you can
   answer, with a priority ranking.

### Pricing tiers

4. **Use anchor pricing with three tiers: Free/Starter, Pro, and a high-touch tier.**

   Free/Starter removes trial friction. Pro is where 80%+ of revenue lives — price at $49-149/month
   for most indie B2B tools. The high-touch tier (Enterprise or Studio) should be priced 3-5x
   Pro and gates collaboration, SSO, SLA, or custom integration features.

   The anchor effect means most buyers self-select into Pro when the Enterprise tier is visibly
   much higher. Lenny's pricing research shows monthly Pro tiers at $49-99 maximize conversion
   rate for SMB B2B.

   Measurable outcome: a visible three-tier pricing page with specific feature differentiators
   per tier — not "everything in Starter plus more."

5. **Locate the paywall at the second or third activation event, not at signup.**

   The activation event is the specific action that causes a user to understand the core value.
   For a deployment tool: first successful deploy. For analytics: first dashboard with real data.

   Paywalling the first event kills conversion. Paywalling too late gives away the value. Find
   the activation event by comparing behavior patterns of paying vs. non-paying users in your
   first 50 signups.

   Measurable outcome: activation event identified from data, and the paywall is placed at the
   second or third recurrence of that event.

6. **Price annually at a 20-25% discount from monthly.**

   Annual pricing improves cash flow, reduces churn risk, and increases LTV. Offer annual plans
   prominently on the pricing page. Most indie SaaS founders who default to monthly-only pricing
   leave 30-40% of potential ARR on the table in years 1-2.

   Pre-select annual on the pricing toggle if your conversion data supports it.

   Measurable outcome: annual plan is available and featured on the pricing page before the
   first paid customer.

### Onboarding to conversion

7. **Reduce time-to-value to under 10 minutes for your free tier.**

   Time-to-value (TTV) is the elapsed time between account creation and the first activation
   event. Every minute of delay is churn risk. Audit your onboarding flow: count steps, form
   fields, and configuration decisions before the user sees a result.

   Remove every step that is not strictly required for the first activation.

   Measurable outcome: a new user can reach the core value moment in under 10 minutes with
   no external assistance.

8. **Send a triggered onboarding email at the 48-hour mark for inactive free users.**

   Users who create an account but do not complete the activation event within 48 hours have
   a 70%+ probability of never converting. A triggered email at 48 hours with a clear next-step
   CTA recovers 10-20% of those users.

   Use a behavioral trigger (did not complete activation event), not a timer-based drip.

   Measurable outcome: 48-hour behavioral trigger email exists in the onboarding sequence,
   with a target click-through rate of 8-12%.

9. **Identify your expansion motion early.**

   Expansion comes from: (a) seat-based growth — more users from the same company, (b) usage-
   based growth — more API calls, data, or projects, or (c) tier upgrades triggered by hitting
   feature limits.

   Design your pricing to make at least one of these paths natural. A product with no expansion
   motion is entirely dependent on new customer acquisition for growth.

   Measurable outcome: the Pro tier has at least one hard limit that a power user will
   realistically hit within 60 days of active use.

### GTM channels

10. **Start with direct outbound to your ICP before SEO or paid ads.**

    For the first 10 customers, SEO takes too long and paid ads require conversion data you do
    not have. Write 50 personalized cold emails using a problem-aware framework:
    (a) specific problem statement matched to the recipient,
    (b) your solution in one sentence,
    (c) a single low-friction ask ("worth a 15-minute call?").

    Personalize the first sentence. Do not send batch-and-blast. Target: 30% open rate, 5%
    reply rate. The first 3 customers from outbound give you the testimonials that make every
    other channel work.

    Measurable outcome: 50 personalized outbound emails sent in the first month, reply data
    tracked in a spreadsheet.

11. **SEO compounds but only for problems people actively search.**

    If your ICP searches Google for their problem before looking for a tool, SEO is your best
    long-term channel. Write content at the "aware of the problem, not yet aware of the
    solution" stage. Use Ahrefs to find 10-20 keywords under 2,000 monthly searches with
    commercial intent — these are winnable without domain authority.

    Measurable outcome: 10 target keywords identified with search volume and difficulty scores
    before the first SEO piece is written.

12. **Build one partnership that gives you direct access to your ICP's existing tool ecosystem.**

    A partnership with a complementary tool (integration, marketplace listing, co-marketing)
    can generate more qualified leads than months of cold outbound. Criteria: (a) their users
    face your problem, (b) integration is technically feasible, (c) their team is small enough
    that a direct founder email gets a response.

    Measurable outcome: at least 1 active integration or co-marketing partnership producing
    at least 5 leads per month.

### Churn signals and expansion

13. **Track leading indicators of churn, not just churn itself.**

    By the time a user cancels, you lost them 2-4 weeks earlier. Leading indicators: login
    frequency drops 50%+ week-over-week, activation event stops occurring, NPS shifts from
    9-10 to 6-7.

    A single check-in email from the founder to an at-risk user recovers 15-25% of at-risk
    accounts in the $50-200/month range.

    Measurable outcome: at least 2 leading churn indicators tracked as metrics with automated
    alerts set.

14. **Track MRR against indie B2B benchmark targets.**

    Reference targets from Lenny's benchmarks for bottom-up B2B without venture funding:

    - First customer: week 1-4
    - $1K MRR: month 2-4
    - $5K MRR: month 6-12
    - $10K MRR: month 12-18
    - $50K MRR (ramen profitable solo): month 18-36

    If you are 6 months behind these targets, diagnose the constraint: wrong ICP, wrong
    pricing, wrong channel, or TTV problem. One root cause drives most stalls.

    Measurable outcome: MRR tracked monthly against this benchmark; gaps diagnosed within 30
    days of falling behind.

## Anti-patterns

- Launching a free tier with no paywall and no activation event tracking. Signups that teach
  you nothing about conversion are operational noise.
- Pricing at $5-10/month for a B2B tool. This attracts the most price-sensitive customers,
  who have the highest support burden and lowest LTV.
- Spending 3+ months building a full marketing website before getting a first customer. The
  first customer comes from a conversation, not a landing page.
- Using churn rate as a lagging metric without leading indicators. You need 2 months of data
  to calculate churn, but leading indicators tell you within days that a customer is at risk.
- Running paid ads before you have a conversion rate from organic traffic. Without an organic
  baseline, you cannot interpret ad data.
- Offering a 14-day free trial in a market where competition offers freemium. A countdown
  clock pressures the user's decision. A free tier lets users expand at their own pace.

## Decision rubric

| Situation | Recommended action |
|---|---|
| Under 10 customers | Direct outbound only; skip SEO and ads |
| Free signups not converting after 30 days | Audit the activation event; move the paywall |
| Churn above 3% monthly MRR | Interview churned accounts before any product changes |
| MRR flat for 2 months | Identify the constraint: acquisition, activation, or expansion |
| Annual vs monthly pricing question | Offer annual at launch; optimize toggle default after 30 days of data |

# Federal Contracting for SDVOSB AI Tooling

## Summary

Covers the registration, certification, and market entry sequence for a service-disabled
veteran-owned small business (SDVOSB) commercializing AI tooling for federal customers. Fires
when a product is being evaluated for federal sales, a registration deadline is approaching, or
a solicitation matching the firm's capabilities appears on SAM.gov.

## When to consult

- SAM.gov registration is expiring or has never been completed.
- A relevant RFI or Sources Sought notice has appeared on SAM.gov.
- A capability statement needs to be drafted or updated before a government meeting.
- An agency is asking about FedRAMP status or ATO timelines.
- SBIR/STTR Phase I solicitation windows are open in a matching technology area.
- A potential teaming arrangement with a large prime is being discussed.
- The annual SAM.gov renewal reminder has fired.

## Recommendations

### Registration prerequisites (before any solicitation response)

1. **Register in SAM.gov and get a UEI before anything else.**

   The Unique Entity Identifier (UEI) replaced DUNS numbers in April 2022. Go to sam.gov,
   create an account, complete entity registration. Allow 10 business days for initial
   validation; plan for up to 3 weeks if there are errors.

   Registration expires exactly 1 year from activation. Set a calendar reminder 60 days
   before expiration. A lapsed SAM.gov registration disqualifies you from award even if you
   win the competition. There is no grace period.

   Measurable outcome: active SAM.gov registration with a confirmed UEI and an expiration
   date in the calendar system.

2. **Complete SDVOSB self-certification, then pursue VA-verified status.**

   As of January 2023, VA CVE verification moved into SAM.gov. The self-certification option
   exists but VA-verified status is required for VA SDVOSB set-asides under the Veterans First
   Contracting Program.

   To VA-certify: upload the VA disability rating letter, DD-214, and business ownership
   documentation (LLC operating agreement showing veteran majority ownership and day-to-day
   management control). Allow 45-90 days for VA review.

   Measurable outcome: SDVOSB status shows as "Verified" in SAM.gov, not "Self-Certified,"
   with the certification expiration date tracked.

3. **Decide on GSA Schedule before pursuing civilian agency contracts above $25K.**

   A GSA Multiple Award Schedule (MAS) contract lets agencies purchase directly without a
   separate procurement action. The application takes 3-6 months and requires 2 years in
   business, past performance documentation, and an accepted price list.

   For AI tooling, relevant SINs include Schedule 70 IT or the Emerging Technology category.
   If the firm is under 2 years old, focus on set-asides and SBIR first. Pursue Schedule after
   the 2-year mark.

   Measurable outcome: GSA Schedule application submitted before year 3, or a documented
   deferral decision with a trigger date.

### Capability statement

4. **Build a one-page capability statement in this exact order.**

   Structure: (1) company logo and tagline, (2) core competencies — 3-5 bullets, specific to
   your technical domain, not "IT services," (3) past performance — match contract type, not
   just technology, (4) differentiators — why you vs. a large integrator: be specific,
   (5) certifications — SDVOSB, CMMC, ISO, SOC2 status as applicable, (6) NAICS codes,
   CAGE code, UEI, and SAM.gov registration date.

   The document must fit on one page. PDF only. Update quarterly.

   Measurable outcome: capability statement is current, has been reviewed by at least one
   contracting officer or small business advisor, and is updated on a quarterly calendar.

5. **Register your NAICS codes correctly.**

   Primary NAICS for AI security tools: 541715 (R&D in Physical/Engineering/Life Sciences),
   541511 (Custom Computer Programming Services), or 541512 (Computer Systems Design Services).

   Add all relevant NAICS codes in SAM.gov — multiple codes are allowed and increase visibility
   in procurement searches. Check size standards for each NAICS — small business set-aside
   eligibility requires revenue under the NAICS-specific threshold.

   Measurable outcome: SAM.gov profile shows at least 3 relevant NAICS codes with correct
   size standard alignment.

### Finding agencies and opportunities

6. **Monitor SAM.gov for RFIs and Sources Sought notices weekly.**

   Set up saved searches on SAM.gov by keyword (AI, machine learning, security, cyber), NAICS
   code, and set-aside type (SDVOSB, 8(a), SBIR). Sources Sought notices are pre-solicitation —
   responding puts your company in the contracting officer's awareness before the formal RFP.

   Respond to every relevant Sources Sought within the response window. The response is 2-4
   pages max: capability statement + specific technical approach to the problem.

   Measurable outcome: at least 3 Sources Sought responses submitted per quarter once
   SAM.gov registration is complete.

7. **Pull agency procurement forecasts from USASpending.gov and agency forecast portals.**

   The DoD publishes an annual procurement forecast; VA and DHS do as well. These list
   contracts expected to compete in the current fiscal year with estimated values and set-aside
   status. Cross-reference against your NAICS codes and set-aside eligibility.

   Focus on contracts in the $250K-$2M range for initial wins. These are below the simplified
   acquisition threshold or in the range where SDVOSB set-asides are actively used.

   Measurable outcome: a tracked list of 5-10 upcoming procurements with estimated award
   dates and assigned follow-up actions.

8. **Target SBIR/STTR Phase I for early-stage AI capabilities.**

   The DoD SBIR program offers Phase I awards of $50K-$300K for proof-of-concept work, with
   Phase II extensions up to $2M. AFWERX (Air Force) and Army xTechSearch are designed to
   engage non-traditional contractors with novel capabilities.

   SBIR topics publish at sbir.defensebusiness.org; application windows are typically 30-45
   days. First submissions rarely win — the decline feedback is more valuable than most
   consulting engagements. Submit to 2-3 topic areas that match your technical roadmap.

   Measurable outcome: first SBIR Phase I application submitted within 90 days of identifying
   a matching topic area.

### Proposal cadence

9. **Do not write full proposals without a gate review.**

   Before investing 40+ hours in a proposal, verify three things:
   (a) You meet every eligibility requirement.
   (b) You have at least one specific differentiator for this requirement.
   (c) You have past performance or a teaming partner that covers the experience requirement.

   A proposal written without all three confirmed is almost always a loss.

   Measurable outcome: a one-page go/no-go decision doc exists for every solicitation before
   full proposal development starts.

10. **Team with a large prime for your first full and open competition above $1M.**

    As a new vendor, your past performance record will not win a competitive full-and-open
    procurement above $1M without an established prime. Find prime contractors who hold existing
    IDIQs (OASIS+, SEWP VI, GSA MAS) in your domain and who need a credible SDVOSB for small
    business subcontracting goals.

    Offer your technical capability in exchange for their capture management infrastructure
    and past performance record.

    Measurable outcome: at least 2 active teaming conversations with established primes within
    6 months of SAM.gov activation.

### FedRAMP 20x path

11. **Pursue FedRAMP 20x for SaaS products targeting civilian agencies.**

    The traditional FedRAMP authorization process costs $1-3M and takes 12-18 months. FedRAMP
    20x (launched 2025) uses automated evidence collection via OSCAL and dramatically reduces
    manual audit overhead. To qualify, the product must use cloud-native infrastructure with
    machine-readable compliance artifacts.

    Start by mapping your existing security controls to the OSCAL template. Engage a 3PAO
    early — their review timeline is the critical path.

    Measurable outcome: OSCAL security plan template populated and reviewed by a 3PAO before
    formal authorization package submission.

## Anti-patterns

- Pursuing a large IDIQ vehicle (OASIS+, SEWP VI) as the first contracting action. These
  require significant past performance and 6-12 months of application work. Year-3 moves,
  not year-1.
- Responding to an RFP without first responding to the Sources Sought for the same requirement.
  You lose the relationship advantage and often miss requirements that RFI respondents shaped.
- Writing a capability statement that describes services generically. Contracting officers read
  hundreds. Specificity wins.
- Assuming SDVOSB self-certification is sufficient for VA set-asides. The Veterans First
  Contracting Program requires VA-verified status.
- Letting SAM.gov registration lapse. No grace period. Cannot receive payment.
- Chasing every apparent opportunity. Proposal effort is expensive. A disciplined go/no-go
  gate is resource allocation, not timidity.

## Decision rubric

| Situation | Recommended action |
|---|---|
| Under 2 years in business | SBIR Phase I and Sources Sought responses; defer GSA Schedule |
| Civilian agency target, SaaS product | Start FedRAMP 20x OSCAL mapping; engage a 3PAO |
| DoD target, edge AI capability | AFWERX SBIR track; Army xTechSearch; OTA consortia |
| Opportunity requires past performance you lack | Find a prime teaming partner or respond with commercial analogues |
| VA opportunity above $25K | Confirm VA-verified SDVOSB status before responding |

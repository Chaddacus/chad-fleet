# LinkedIn Algorithm Playbook (2026)

## Summary

Covers the mechanics of the LinkedIn 2025-2026 feed algorithm and how to structure posts for
maximum organic reach. Specific to an individual creator doing thought-leadership and launch
content, not a brand page. Fires when a post is being drafted, a launch is being planned on
LinkedIn, or reach has dropped unexpectedly.

## When to consult

- A post needs to reach beyond your immediate follower base.
- A product launch, OSS release, or article is being distributed on LinkedIn.
- Recent posts have plateaued below 500 impressions on a 2K+ follower account.
- A content calendar is being built and post format needs to be standardized.
- You are deciding between a native document, a GIF, a text post, or a video.
- A campaign post is being planned and timing needs to be optimized.

## Recommendations

### Dwell-time mechanics

1. **Write posts that earn 61+ seconds of dwell time.**

   LinkedIn's algorithm uses dwell time — the amount of time a user spends viewing a post —
   as the primary quality signal. A post that earns 61+ seconds of active reading triggers a
   reach multiplier estimated at 8-13x over posts that users scroll past in under 5 seconds.

   The implication: a short post with no depth punishes you even if it gets likes. Write posts
   that reward the reader for stopping. A 500-word framework post consistently outperforms a
   3-sentence "thought" in reach.

   Measurable outcome: track post reach; posts with substantive comments (not just reactions)
   signal high dwell time indirectly. Aim for average comment length of 10+ words.

2. **Optimize for saves, not likes.**

   Saves (bookmark icon) and substantive comments (5+ words) are weighted more heavily than
   reactions in the 2025-2026 algorithm. A post with 200 saves and 10 comments outperforms a
   post with 500 likes and 2 saves.

   Design posts with ongoing utility — frameworks, checklists, and reference posts earn saves.
   Saves drive re-distribution through "content you might want to revisit" prompts that
   LinkedIn surfaces to the original saver's connections.

   Measurable outcome: at least 1 save per 50 views on a well-performing post.

### Hook construction

3. **The first line must earn the "see more" click.**

   LinkedIn shows 2-3 lines before truncating with "...see more." The first 1-2 sentences are
   the only content competing for the scroll. The hook must: name a specific tension, promise
   a concrete payoff, or make a counterintuitive claim the reader can verify in the next 30
   seconds.

   Do not start with: "I'm excited to share," "In today's world," or "Storytelling has been
   around since..." These are reach-killing openers. Write the hook last, after the body is
   complete, so you know exactly what payoff you are promising.

   Measurable outcome: every post draft gets a hook review before publishing — the first 2
   lines must stand alone as a reason to click.

4. **Keep each line under 49 characters.**

   LinkedIn's mobile feed wraps at approximately 49 characters. Lines longer than that break
   mid-word or mid-phrase, which disrupts reading cadence. Write every line to wrap cleanly
   at a natural pause.

   This is not aesthetics — a broken line at the "see more" threshold loses clicks. Test by
   pasting your hook into a character counter before publishing.

   Measurable outcome: every line preceding "see more" is under 49 characters.

5. **Use a line break between every 1-2 sentences.**

   Dense paragraphs are invisible in the LinkedIn feed on mobile. White space is what makes
   content readable at a scroll. Standard structure: 1 sentence → blank line → 1-2 sentences
   → blank line → continuation.

   Post bodies written as prose paragraphs get ignored. The visual impression of "readable"
   must be established in 0.5 seconds.

   Measurable outcome: no paragraph in a LinkedIn post exceeds 2 sentences without a blank
   line break.

### Post shape and content type

6. **Match your post shape to the content type.**

   Four patterns that work in 2026:

   - **Framework post**: here is a model I use to make decision X. Structure: problem →
     3-5 labeled components → when to apply it. Earns saves.
   - **Lesson post**: here is what I learned from doing Y. Structure: the mistake or
     experience → the specific lesson → how it changed my behavior. Earns comments.
   - **Insight post**: here is a data point that surprised me. Structure: the finding →
     why it matters → what to do with it. Earns shares.
   - **Example post**: here is a real case study with numbers. Structure: context → action →
     specific result (dollar amount, time saved, percentage change). Earns saves and shares.

   Do not mix types in a single post. Before writing, name which of the four types the post is.

   Measurable outcome: every published post maps to one of these four types in the content log.

7. **Native documents (carousels or PDFs) earn amplified distribution.**

   LinkedIn's algorithm continues to favor native document uploads over plain text or external
   link posts. A 5-10 slide PDF that teaches a framework or checklist earns 2-3x the impressions
   of a plain text post at comparable hook quality.

   The first slide is the thumbnail — treat it like a cover. Readable at 150x150 pixels. Bold
   text, high contrast, single headline.

   Measurable outcome: at least 1 native document post per month in a regular content schedule.

8. **Native video above 60 seconds earns dwell time directly.**

   Native LinkedIn video (not a YouTube link) benefits from the dwell-time algorithm directly.
   Talking-head videos with a specific lesson outperform B-roll. Add captions — 80% of LinkedIn
   video is watched on mute. Do not start with a logo animation or "hey everyone, welcome back"
   — that costs the dwell in the first 8 seconds.

   Open with the core claim or demonstration in the first 8 seconds.

   Measurable outcome: if publishing video, the first frame shows the claim, not an intro.

### Comment-triggered amplification

9. **Reply to every comment within the first 60 minutes.**

   Comments restart the reach distribution cycle. When you reply, it resurfaces the post in
   the commenter's network feed. The first hour after posting is when LinkedIn samples engagement
   rate against a subset of your followers. High engagement in that window triggers distribution
   to second-degree connections.

   If you are not actively commenting in the first hour, you are manually capping your reach.

   Measurable outcome: calendar block the 60 minutes immediately after posting for comment
   engagement.

10. **Ask a specific question at the end of the post.**

    Not "what do you think?" — that generates no signal. The question should have a short,
    opinionated answer that invites a concrete response or mild disagreement.

    Examples that work: "Which of these 3 patterns do you default to?" or "Has this tradeoff
    come up in your team?"

    Polarizing but professional questions generate 3-5x more comment volume than open-ended
    generic prompts.

    Measurable outcome: every post ends with a 1-sentence specific question.

### Posting cadence and timing

11. **Post 3-4 times per week at maximum.**

    Posting more than once per day suppresses reach on subsequent posts — LinkedIn throttles
    accounts that post too frequently by reducing distribution on same-day posts. Consistency
    over 90 days builds the algorithm's model of your audience more effectively than volume.

    Never post twice in the same calendar day.

    Measurable outcome: posting log shows no two posts within the same calendar day.

12. **Post between 7-9 AM or 12-1 PM in your audience's primary timezone.**

    These windows reflect LinkedIn feed traffic peaks for professional audiences in North America.
    Tuesday through Thursday outperform Monday and Friday. Posts published after 6 PM local
    time have limited engagement in the first 60 minutes, capping algorithmic distribution.

    Measurable outcome: all scheduled posts land in one of the two time windows on a weekday.

## Anti-patterns

- Writing posts as 3+ paragraphs of unbroken prose. Mobile readers see a wall of text and
  scroll past. This is the single most common failure mode for smart people who write well.
- Using 5+ hashtags per post. The 2024-2026 algorithm reduced hashtag reach multiplication.
  1-2 specific hashtags. More than 3 signals keyword stuffing.
- Posting an external link in the post body. Off-platform links get suppressed. Put external
  links in the first comment.
- Engagement baiting with vague questions. Single-character comment responses do not trigger
  the reach amplification that substantive comments do.
- Reposting the exact same content within 30 days. LinkedIn's duplicate content filter
  suppresses reposts. Repurpose with a different hook and angle.
- Tagging 10+ people in a post for reach. Mass-tagging reads as spam to both the algorithm
  and the tagged people.
- Writing about feelings instead of insights. "I'm so grateful for this journey" posts from
  personal brands under 10K followers reach nobody.

## Decision rubric

| Situation | Recommended action |
|---|---|
| Post under 300 impressions | Audit the hook — it failed to earn the see-more click |
| Post got likes but no saves | Content was entertaining but not useful; add a framework or checklist |
| Comments are all "great post!" | End question was too vague; tighten to a specific choice |
| Video underperformed text | First 8 seconds lost the audience; open with the claim |
| Reach dropped across all posts | Check frequency and hashtag count; likely over-posting or over-tagging |
